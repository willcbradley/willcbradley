What Is Medicine?

Healthcare is highly regulated.

It's not hard to see why: high-stakes decisions demand high-strength guardrails. When my life's on the line, I don't want my doctor winging it.

But there are side effects.

Regulation insulates us from reality, replacing meritocracy with status games. And often, status games can become warped, bloated, and zero-sum.

The upshot?

Succeeding as a doctor requires a different skillset to succeeding in the free market. And as technological change speeds up, this difference will only grow.

This leaves today's doctors in a rough spot.

On one hand, they could go all-in on the medical game. This makes them entirely reliant on regulators, but at least they'll have a shot at getting onto specialty training.

On the other hand, they could neglect the game, choosing instead to learn skills that let them survive in the market. But if that's the goal, why bother with a medical degree?

It's a catch-22.

Those who play by the rules risk tech-driven redundancy, and those who reject them risk being chewed up and spat out by winner-take-all markets.

We're being pulled in two opposing directions at once. Each step forward is also a step backward.

Unless we're missing something.

If medicine is nothing but a series of exams, hospitals, and training programs, then we remain trapped. If it's purely a way of paying the bills, the same is true.

But what if there's something more durable?

Something that's universally present, no matter the situation? Something we can always count on, for as long as humans exist?

I spent a long time looking, and the only constant I could find is suffering. This might sound pessimistic, but I'm not so sure.

After all, what if medicine was the open-eyed acceptance of inevitable suffering, and the decision to work against it anyway?

What if we held this ideal above all else, no matter what?

Maybe this involves certain career milestones. Maybe it involves operating in the free market. But these become secondary, interchangeable processes in service of the main goal.

If we get a nice job title in the process, that's great. If we can afford a few extra Porsches, all the better.

But the faster the world moves, the more fundamental our motivations need to be.

And unless we figure this out in time, I doubt tomorrow's doctors will be able to save themselves, let alone their patients.

Seems worth a try.
