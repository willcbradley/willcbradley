How Opportunities Work

There are two fundamental sources of error correction: nature and human nature. No matter what, we can test ourselves against these standards, and develop timeless skills. No work needs to be wasted.

But of course, some kinds of work yield more return than others.

Opportunities, by definition, are force multipliers on effort. They're not a replacement for work, but a means of enhancing it.

How do they work?

The primary purpose of opportunities is to create more opportunities. The whole point of today is to make tomorrow more exciting, unless we're cashing in our chips.

This works because any given opportunity builds our track record, which makes us eligible for new, better ones. To capitalise on this, we reinvest all excess resources (above and beyond current demands) towards new opportunities with higher risk-adjusted return than our current options.

Corollary: the higher our burn rate of money and time, the less we can invest in new opportunities. Eventually, we hit an equilibrium where our excess bandwidth is less than the cost of better opportunities. Then, we stagnate.

How can this go wrong?

Unjustified burn rate. Things that limit our investment in new opportunities, without providing a net benefit elsewhere.

Think of the person who builds their career on status games. They can't cut their burn rate to reinvest in better opportunities, because without social approval, they are nothing. The pain of this loss is too great to justify any attempts at real work.

How to avoid this?

Rely on skill. Play status games as needed, but only in service of fundamentals. Be ready to shed prestige at a moment's notice.

With this approach, we can play indefinitely. Instead of cashing in, we can leave our chips on the table as a bankroll for future generations.

What could be more worthwhile than that?
